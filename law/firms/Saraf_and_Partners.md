## Firm Overview

Saraf and Partners is a full-service Indian commercial law firm founded in July 2021 by Mohit Saraf, the co-founder of Luthra & Luthra Law Offices (subsequently renamed L&L Partners). The firm launched as one of the most dramatic breakaway events in modern Indian legal history, with 21 partners departing L&L Partners to constitute its founding team.

Within three years of operation, the firm grew from a founding strength of approximately 84 lawyers across three cities to over 250 professionals, including 38 partners, across five offices. By 2024, it had executed deals worth a claimed US$56 billion.

The firm describes its positioning as "Smart Law" — combining deep legal acumen with technology-driven processes, strategic clarity, and a client-centric approach. It operates as an independent, non-partnership-owned entity with an equity distribution model that allocated one-third to the founder (Mohit Saraf), one-third to departing founding partners, and one-third reserved for future talent.

The firm targets complex, high-stakes transactions and litigation, competing directly against Tier 1 Indian firms such as AZB & Partners, Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas, Khaitan & Co., and Shardul Amarchand Mangaldas.

## Organizational Hierarchy

The firm operates a tiered partnership structure typical of elite Indian commercial law firms:

```
Founder & Managing Partner
    └── Management Committee (Senior Partners)
            └── Senior Partners
                    └── Partners
                            └── Partner Designates
                                    └── Senior Associates
                                            └── Associates
                                                    └── Interns / Trainees
```

- **Founder & Managing Partner:** Mohit Saraf (sole decision-maker on major strategic questions)
- **Management Committee:** Vaibhav Kakkar, Manmeet Singh (until departure to CAM in late 2024), Bikash Jhawar, and others
- **Senior Partners:** Sanjeev Sharma, Vikrant Kumar, Bikash Jhawar, Vaibhav Kakkar, Saroj Kumar Jha, Ravi Bishnoi

## Leadership & Management

### Mohit Saraf — Founder & Managing Partner

Mohit Saraf is one of the most prominent transactional lawyers in India. He co-founded Luthra & Luthra Law Offices in 1999 (later renamed L&L Partners) and built it over 20 years from a boutique into a firm of 350 lawyers. A dispute with co-founder Rajiv Luthra over ownership sharing with younger partners led to litigation before the Delhi High Court. The parties reached a settlement in June 2021, with Saraf exiting L&L upon receipt of approximately USD 7 million, and subsequently founding Saraf and Partners in July 2021.

## Technology & Tools

Saraf and Partners publicly espouses a "Smart Law" philosophy that emphasizes technology integration, though specific tools are not publicly disclosed. Based on firm communications and industry context, the following can be reasonably attributed:

- **Document Management:** Standard enterprise document management systems used by mid-to-large Indian law firms (likely iManage or similar).
- **Legal Research:** Westlaw India / SCC Online / Manupatra (standard Indian legal research databases).
- **Communication:** The firm has been noted in employee and intern reviews to use **WhatsApp** heavily for internal communications, including policy announcements from senior leadership — a practice that has drawn some criticism for its informality.
- **Video collaboration:** Standard tools (Zoom, Microsoft Teams) for client and inter-office communication.
- **AI/LegalTech:** In October 2023 (Annual Day), Mohit Saraf highlighted AI's transformative impact on legal services and the firm's "commitment to leveraging technological advancements." No specific AI tool or vendor partnership has been publicly named.
- **Billing and matter management:** Not publicly disclosed.

The firm's internship experience reviews note that interns were used for due diligence procedures and research tasks, suggesting reliance on human resource rather than heavily automated workflows at the junior level — with some criticism that interns were used as "ChatGPT prompt engineers rather than lawyers."

## Work Processes & Methodology

Based on public information, client feedback in rankings, and associate/intern reviews:

**Client service model:**
- The firm is positioned as a "complex matters" specialist. It competes on quality and seniority of partner involvement rather than price competition.
- Chambers client feedback references "tight timelines, excellent knowledge across matters, expertise in handling complex transactions."
- The firm emphasizes partner-led advice, with Mohit Saraf reportedly being personally involved in marquee transactions.

**Transaction workflow:**
- Large transactions are typically led by a Senior or founding Partner, with a team of 2–5 associates below.
- Real estate and insolvency teams (post-SRGR merger) operate relatively semi-autonomously under Saroj Kumar Jha and Ravi Bishnoi.
- The dispute resolution team historically operated under Manmeet Singh until his departure to CAM in late 2024; this has created a significant gap in the litigation practice.

**Internal communication:**
- Internal policies are heavily centralized around the Managing Partner. Multiple sources indicate ad hoc policy-making via WhatsApp groups.
- The management committee includes select senior partners but ultimate authority rests with Mohit Saraf.

**Work culture:**
- The firm operates a demanding, high-volume environment typical of elite transactional Indian law firms.
- Glassdoor ratings: Work-life balance 3.1/5; Culture & Values 3.7/5; Career Opportunities 4.0/5; overall 4.0/5 (21 reviews).
- 78% of employees would recommend the firm to a friend (Glassdoor).
- The firm maintains a claimed 50:50 gender ratio among lawyers — an unusual achievement in Indian commercial law.

## Training & Knowledge Management

**Formal programs:**
- The firm has a stated commitment to "continuous learning and development," mentorship, and creating pathways to leadership.
- Mohit Saraf has personally emphasized "mentorship and relationship-building as cornerstones for cultivating the next leadership echelon" (Annual Day 2023 speech).
- The firm conducts **POSH and gender sensitization sessions** across all offices.
- Intern onboarding includes brief interactive sessions by associates covering employability skills.

**Informal training:**
- Senior partners are described in intern reviews as being "patient when explaining nuances of contract drafting" and teaching through live cases.
- The firm's preference for hiring from top-4 NLUs and JGLS (National Law Schools) and Jindal Global Law School skews training toward a self-selecting cohort of well-prepared lawyers.

**Internship program:**
- Applications are primarily by mail to HR.
- Stipend: Approximately ₹6,000–₹20,000/month depending on practice area and role (corporate communication internships reported at ₹20,000/month; legal internships at ₹6,000).
- PPOs (Pre-Placement Offers) are reportedly available but have been described in Legally India forums as rare in practice.
- The Noida office accepts interns for corporate and legal work; the firm advertises internships through Lawctopus and law school notice boards.

**Knowledge management critique:**
- There is no publicly described formal knowledge management system (KM portal, precedent bank system, etc.). Given the firm's youth (founded 2021) and rapid growth, a mature KM infrastructure is unlikely to exist compared to firms like AZB or Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas.
- Interns have noted being assigned to due diligence tasks with limited mentorship, suggesting KM is primarily ad hoc and partner-driven.

## Client Complaints & Criticism

### Internal Employee Complaints (Glassdoor / Legally India)

**Compensation and salary compression:**
- Documented concern that the firm hires fresh graduates (A0s) at higher starting salaries than existing A1–A3 associates hired in prior years, creating internal compression. No corresponding increments or appraisals were provided to existing lawyers.
- Bonuses reportedly paid 6 months late "without fail," with "any excuse possible" used to reduce or cut bonus amounts before payment.

**Leave and work-life policy:**
- No written leave policy provided to new joiners at onboarding.
- The firm's "sandwich leave policy" penalizes employees for public holidays falling adjacent to approved leave days.
- Work-from-home restricted to Wednesdays only (as of 2022–23), announced via WhatsApp rather than formal HR communication.

**Management and governance:**
- Multiple reviews describe "no firm policies" and "policies created on the spot as per the managing partner's whims and fancies."
- Decisions about compensation, leave, and work structure are highly centralized.
- One Glassdoor review (2024) titled "Toxic Environment" alleged that individuals who left the firm did so "on horrible terms" with final settlements contested on "baseless accusations."

**Hiring bias:**
- Significant hiring preference for graduates of NLSIU (Bangalore), NALSAR (Hyderabad), NLU Delhi, NLU Kolkata, and Jindal Global Law School (JGLS). Meritorious students from other institutions reportedly face dismissive treatment.
- Interns from non-top-tier institutions describe being assigned to mechanical tasks rather than substantive legal work.

**Attrition concerns:**
- A Legally India forum commenter described the firm as "pretty much a dead firm and not offering PPOs" — though this characterization is contested and likely reflects a moment-in-time view from 2022–23.
- Internal politics noted by multiple sources; multiple senior departures occurred between 2022 and 2025 (Gayatri Roy, Manmeet Singh's team of four).

**Claims credibility issue:**
- A Legally India thread questioned the firm claiming awards recognition back to 2018 in marketing materials, despite having been founded in July 2021. This appears to relate to Mohit Saraf personally carrying his personal recognition from his L&L tenure into the firm's branding — technically accurate for an individual but potentially misleading as a firm claim.

### External / Market Criticism

- The firm's dispute resolution practice was materially weakened by the December 2024 departure of Manmeet Singh and three colleagues to Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas. This followed the earlier disputes-team departure and raises questions about the stickiness of the disputes practice.
- Mathew Thomas, who had been hired to build the capital markets practice, departed to CMS INDUSLAW in November 2025 — suggesting retention challenges even for lateral hires.
- The firm has no significant disputes ranking in Chambers despite Tier 4 positioning in Legal 500, indicating it is not yet a credible Tier 1 competitor in litigation/arbitration.

### Positive Counterpoints

- 78% of Glassdoor respondents would recommend the firm to a friend.
- The firm's 50:50 gender ratio is genuinely unusual for Indian commercial law and represents a meaningful cultural achievement.
- Pro bono and CSR commitments include providing legal services to those who cannot afford representation.
- Career opportunities rated 4.0/5 on Glassdoor, suggesting genuine upward mobility for high performers.

**Competitive positioning notes:**

1. **Strengths vs. peers:** Saraf and Partners is most credibly positioned in M&A, restructuring/IBC, and energy/infrastructure projects — the three areas where Mohit Saraf has personal brand equity. The SRGR acquisition gave it a genuine competitive position in real estate.

2. **Weaknesses vs. peers:** The loss of the Manmeet Singh disputes team to CAM in 2024 effectively ended the firm's credibility as a full-service disputes shop for the near term. Capital markets similarly weakened with Thomas's departure to CMS INDUSLAW.

3. **Partnership model advantage:** The firm's equity structure (one-third each to founder, founding partners, and future talent) was designed to avoid the governance breakdown that destroyed the Saraf-Luthra partnership at L&L. However, early departures of senior figures like Gayatri Roy and Manmeet Singh suggest structural tensions persist.

4. **Growth trajectory:** In three years the firm went from under 100 to 250+ lawyers and claimed US$56 billion in deal value. This is a remarkable growth rate by any measure. Whether it can be sustained without the departure of more key rainmakers is the central strategic question.

5. **Market perception:** The firm is regarded as a credible alternative to the legacy Tier 1 firms for M&A, restructuring, and projects mandates but has not yet achieved the client-conflict-free market depth, institutional client base, or deal pipeline depth of AZB or Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas.
