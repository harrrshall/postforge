## Firm Overview

Trilegal is a leading full-service Indian law firm founded in **2000** by six lawyers who merged three start-up practices based in Delhi, Bangalore, and Mumbai. The firm's founding was driven not by profit maximization but by a deliberate ambition to create India's first truly meritocratic, transparent, and institutionally governed law firm — a counterpoint to the family-owned and proprietorial firms that dominated the Indian market at the time.

As of early 2026, Trilegal fields **over 1,200 lawyers** (professionals including associates, senior associates, and counsel) across **eight office locations** in India, with an **all-equity partnership of approximately 150 partners**. The firm describes itself as the **largest equity partnership in India** and is unique among top Indian firms in operating on a pure lockstep model, where all partners are equity-holding owners.

### History

- **2000**: Founded by six lawyers — Akshay Jaitly, Anand Prasad, Karan Singh, Prem Ayyappa, Rahul Matthan, and Sridhar Gorthi — via a three-way merger of Delhi, Bangalore, and Mumbai start-ups. Launched from basements and living rooms with revenues under ₹2 crore in year one.
- **2007**: Advised Vodafone (alongside Linklaters) on the $11.1 billion acquisition of a 67% stake in Hutchison Essar — one of the largest M&A deals ever in India.
- **2008**: Entered a non-financial referral/training relationship with Allen & Overy (ended 2012).
- **2016**: Co-founding partner Anand Prasad left the partnership (amicably) to pursue counsel practice and politics.
- **2020**: Management transition — founders stepped back from day-to-day management; Management Committee handed over to Nishant Parikh and Sridhar Gorthi. Supervisory Board established.
- **2024**: Revenue crossed ₹1,000 crore for the first time; 11 new partners promoted in a single round (record).
- **2025**: Akshay Jaitly publishes *Trilegal: The Making of a Modern Indian Law Firm* — the first time an Indian law firm has publicly disclosed revenue metrics. Equity partnership grows to 150.

## Organizational Hierarchy

Trilegal's governance is explicitly designed to prevent founder capture and replicate a democratic institution rather than a traditional family-run law firm.

### Governance Layers

```
Partnership (all equity, ~150 partners)
        │
        ▼
Supervisory Board (10 partners)
  — 3 co-founding partners (Matthan, Singh, Jaitly)
  — 7 non-founder elected partners
        │
        ▼
Management Committee (2 members)
  — Nishant Parikh (Chair)
  — Sridhar Gorthi
        │
        ▼
Practice Heads (per practice area)
        │
        ▼
Office Heads (per geographic location)
        │
        ▼
Partners → Counsel → Senior Associates → Associates (A0–A3, SA1–SA3)
```

### Key Features of the Structure

- **One Partner, One Vote**: Every equity partner has equal voting rights regardless of seniority.
- **Management Committee (MC)**: Two-member body responsible for day-to-day management. Elected by the partnership. Members serve 3-year terms; maximum of two consecutive terms.
- **Supervisory Board (SB)**: Ten-partner body overseeing the MC. Includes founding partners but a majority are non-founders.
- **Practice Heads**: Individual partners designated as heads for each major practice area.
- **Office Heads**: Designated partner responsible for each geographic office.
- **No founding equity reservation**: Founders can be theoretically outvoted; new partners can rise to the same lockstep level as founders.

## Work Processes & Methodology

### Client Service Approach

Trilegal positions itself on a **commercial and solution-oriented** model:

- Lawyers are trained to approach every matter with a client's **business objective** in mind, not just legal correctness.
- The firm applies **cross-practice integration**: for example, the arbitration team works in tandem with corporate, banking, restructuring, regulatory, and white-collar crime practices to give holistic advice.
- The disputes practice devises **matter-specific litigation strategies** aligned with clients' commercial objectives rather than following a formulaic approach.

### Team Structure

- **Partner-led structured teams** for every practice area. Partners are actively involved in matters, not just at origination.
- Cross-office collaboration is standard for large transactions; partner might be based in Delhi but work with a Mumbai team.
- High intensity matters are acknowledged openly — "the hours are long, the demands are intense, the stakes are high" — but this is positioned as counterbalanced by collegial culture.

### Transaction Process

- Full-service transactional support from term sheet to closing.
- Integrated M&A + Tax + Regulatory approach for complex deals.
- Dedicated teams for regulatory clearances (CCI, SEBI, RBI, NCLT).

## Client Complaints & Criticism

### Work Culture: Employee Reviews (Glassdoor, Legally India)

Trilegal's overall Glassdoor rating is approximately **3.8/5** based on multiple reviews, with **2.9/5 for work-life balance** — below its score for compensation (4.3/5). The pattern that emerges from reviews is a firm with excellent compensation and intellectually stimulating work, but with significant and well-documented culture inconsistencies by team/practice:

**Recurring Critical Themes**:

1. **Intensity and hours**: Work is acknowledged as extremely intense. Calls at 8 pm on Saturdays, 6–7 hour video calls to discuss edits, and weekend work without advance notice are cited by multiple reviewers.

2. **Inconsistent seniority culture**: Some reviewers describe being shouted at by seniors, personal jibes, and a blame culture ("pushing each other under the bus"). One review explicitly called it "toxic even by standards of corporate India."

3. **Warring partners**: Forum comments on Legally India reference "warring partner factions" that create a "war zone-like environment" for associates caught in the middle.

4. **Attrition**: High vacancy counts are cited as evidence of high attrition, which participants attribute to the work culture rather than external opportunities.

5. **Compensation as compensation**: Critics argue that the elevated salary (₹19.6L for freshers) is not evidence of a good workplace but rather compensation for difficult conditions — i.e., "they are paying you for the torture."

6. **Political culture**: Some reviewers note that if personal views do not align with a senior's opinions, there are informal mechanisms to sideline or make life difficult for dissenting associates.

**Positive Feedback**:

- Compensation is consistently rated highly.
- Work quality and deal exposure are described as excellent.
- The firm is not family-owned, which some employees value for transparency and meritocracy.
- Management is described as "approachable" and the firm as "professional" by satisfied employees.
- Experiences vary significantly by team and location — some teams receive high praise.

### Structural Criticism

- **Partnership admission as management**: The 2020 management transition was noted by insiders as more forced than voluntary — some accounts suggest the partnership effectively compelled the founders to step aside rather than the founders graciously doing so.
- **The lockstep floor**: While praised as meritocratic, some counsel note that new equity partners begin at the lowest lockstep rung regardless of prior experience or client relationships brought to the firm, which may discourage lateral senior hires.

### Trilegal's Differentiators

1. **Lockstep model**: Only major Indian firm with a full all-equity lockstep. This is both a philosophical and structural differentiator — promoted as more democratic and meritocratic than the equity concentration at family-run competitors.
2. **No founding equity reserve**: Founders can reach the same lockstep maximum as newer partners — and could theoretically be outvoted.
3. **Technology leadership**: The Microsoft/Azure/Lucio stack places Trilegal ahead of most Indian peers on AI adoption.
4. **Revenue growth rate**: Between 2020–2025, Trilegal's revenue roughly quadrupled while the industry average (top 6 firms) grew 83% in headcount terms. Trilegal tripled its headcount in the same period.
5. **Disputes strength**: Chambers Band 1 for arbitration and white-collar crime — areas where some competitors are weaker.

### Market Position

The India legal services market is **moderately fragmented**. The top five firms hold slightly over one-fourth of total market share. The broader market was valued at approximately **$1.3 billion** as of recent estimates, with strong growth projected to 2030 driven by FDI inflows, infrastructure investment, M&A activity, and regulatory complexity.

Trilegal is generally ranked **5th by size** (Cyril Amarchand, AZB, Shardul, Khaitan being larger or co-equal) but increasingly claims the #1 position on specific metrics — revenue per lawyer, deal ranking table positions, and equity partnership size.
