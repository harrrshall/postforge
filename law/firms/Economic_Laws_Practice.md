## Firm Overview

Economic Laws Practice (ELP) was founded in 2001 by four partners — Rohan Shah, Suhail Nathani, Sujjain Talwar, and Vikram Nankani — with a stated mission of bringing together a diverse set of professionals (lawyers, chartered accountants, cost accountants, economists, and company secretaries) to deliver integrated, practical legal solutions.

ELP positions itself as a "full-service Tier 1 law firm," though in market positioning it is generally understood as a strong Tier 2 firm with Tier 1 depth in its core specialties: tax, trade remedies/customs, and competition. The firm has grown from the original four founders to approximately **54 partners** and **300+ qualified professionals** across eight offices.

A distinctive feature of ELP is its multi-disciplinary staffing model: many of its lawyers hold dual qualifications as lawyers and chartered accountants (CA), company secretaries (CS), or economists. This is particularly evident in the tax and regulatory practices, which benefit from integrated CA/CS/legal expertise.

ELP is **India's representative firm in the Taxand global network** — the world's largest independent tax organisation with 700+ tax partners and 3,000+ advisors across 48 countries. This membership gives ELP cross-border reach on international tax mandates.

The firm's stated client base spans Fortune 500 multinationals, public sector undertakings, Indian conglomerates, mid-market corporates, and start-ups. Sectors include mining, commodities, engineering and construction, shipping, insurance, infrastructure, pharmaceuticals, real estate, technology, and financial services.

**RSG Report 2019** ranked ELP among the top ten law firms in India, with the **highest client satisfaction rating (9.1) among comparable firms** in that survey period.

## Organizational Hierarchy

ELP uses a structured career ladder modelled in part after the Big Four accounting firms, which reflects its multi-disciplinary DNA:

```
Equity Partner ("Custodian")
        |
    Partner
        |
  Associate Partner
        |
  Senior Associate / Associate Manager
        |
     Associate
        |
  Trainee / Articled Clerk / Intern
```

**Key structural features:**

- **"Custodian" model:** Equity partners are referred to as "custodians" of the firm — a governance concept emphasizing stewardship over ownership. As of 2024, there are approximately 20+ equity partners.
- **Partner (non-equity / salaried):** A significant tier of lateral hires and promotions who are partners in title but not equity-sharing.
- **Associate Partner:** A distinct pre-partner tier, equivalent to "Counsel" or "Senior Associate" at other firms.
- **Associate Managers:** A step created to mirror CA-firm structures, sitting between senior associates and associate partners.
- **Dual-qualification requirement:** The firm actively recruits and promotes professionals who hold both a law degree and a CA/CS/economics qualification, particularly for tax and regulatory roles.

**Governance:** The firm underwent significant governance restructuring after founding partner Rohan Shah retired in September 2016, transitioning from founder-led management to a broader equity partnership. Shah described this as "reinforcing our original commitment" by moving leadership to the next generation. Suhail Nathani assumed the Managing Partner role and has held it since.

In 2024, the firm adopted a more formalized promotion cycle, announcing 7 new equity stakeholders simultaneously — a departure from piecemeal annual promotions — signalling a more structured partnership track.

## Work Processes & Methodology

ELP's working methodology is distinguished by several features:

### 1. Litigation-Informed Advisory
ELP lawyers are required, as part of their development, to appear in tribunals, courts, and regulatory forums. This means even transactional and advisory lawyers have courtroom exposure, which the firm argues produces more practically useful advice — particularly important for tax structuring where understanding the litigation risk is critical.

### 2. Multi-Disciplinary Teams
Most senior fee-earners in tax and regulatory practices hold dual qualifications (law degree + CA, CS, or economics). This allows the firm to deliver integrated financial and legal analysis without handoffs. Client testimonials frequently cite this as a differentiator.

### 3. Cross-Practice Collaboration
The firm emphasises seamless cross-practice integration. A complex M&A with customs and competition dimensions, for example, would draw simultaneously from the corporate, tax, customs, and competition teams under a unified partner oversight structure.

### 4. Partner-Led Delivery
A recurring theme in client feedback is direct partner involvement at all stages. Client testimonials specifically praise availability of senior partners: *"I rank ELP as the top firm in terms of team and organisation quality"* and *"timely, responsive, and practical advice"* from Rohit Jain.

### 5. Long-Term Client Relationships
The firm's stated commercial philosophy is to build long-term client relationships rather than transactional engagements. This is reflected in their retainer structures across tax compliance, GST advisory, and trade law.

### 6. Taxand Network Leveraging
For international tax mandates (inbound investment structuring, cross-border M&A tax, transfer pricing), ELP coordinates through the Taxand network, giving clients access to local tax counsel in 48 countries through a single point of contact in India.

## Client Complaints & Criticism

### 1. SEBI Conflict of Interest (2020–2021)

The most prominent public controversy surrounding ELP concerns a conflict of interest dispute with SEBI. The Securities and Exchange Board of India sent an email to ELP requesting the firm return SEBI's legal briefs in various matters after ELP agreed to represent Essel Group Chairman Subhash Chandra and Zee Entertainment CEO Punit Goenka against SEBI before the Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT). The duo was challenging an interim SEBI order barring them from holding directorial positions in listed companies.

SEBI's position was based on its empanelment guidelines for advocates, which restrict empanelled firms from acting against the regulator (with exemptions for Senior Advocates).

ELP's Managing Partner Suhail Nathani defended the firm publicly: *"It is the client's absolute prerogative to appoint a lawyer of its choice. Clients and lawyers are mutually free to set their own standards of engagement within the confines of professional rules."*

The incident raised broader questions about the conflict-management practices of large Indian law firms that simultaneously serve regulators and regulated entities. ELP's regulatory practice had been representing SEBI for many years; the firm was effectively forced to choose sides. This episode highlighted a structural vulnerability in firms with both government/regulatory advisory work and corporate enforcement-defence work.

### 2. Partner Attrition and Team Departures

ELP has experienced several notable group departures:

- **August 2020:** Equity partner Darshan Upadhyay and corporate partners Shyam Pandya, Amit Manubarwala, and Bhavin Gada simultaneously left to found **Stratage Law Partners** — a significant group departure from the corporate practice.
- **December 2020:** Partner Tushar Ajinkya and associate partner Ankita Kashyap (plus three others) left to establish **ThinkLaw**, a boutique corporate firm in Mumbai.
- **July 2019:** Indirect tax partners MP Devnath and Vivek Sharma, plus associate partner Abhishek Anand, departed together to join **Sarvada Legal**.
- **June 2019:** Former partner Deep Roy founded **Equilex**, focusing on finance and insolvency.
- **2021:** Associate partner Faraz Sagar left ELP to join **Trilegal** as a partner.
- **2019:** Partner Ashutosh Gupta joined **HSA Advocates** as partner and chief strategy officer.
- **2012:** Competition partner Samir Gandhi left to join **AZB & Partners**, where he became a founding force in their competition practice.

These departures, particularly the group exits, suggest recurring friction around equity access, governance, and commercials in the non-tax practices.

### 3. Employee Feedback Themes (Glassdoor)

From 25 anonymous employee reviews (4.1/5 overall):

**Recurring negatives:**
- *"Extensive working hours"* — multiple reviewers cite long hours as a significant downside, particularly for associates and senior associates.
- *"Politics in promotion"* — advancement decisions perceived as opaque or influenced by internal politics rather than pure meritocracy.
- *"Lack of structure"* — as professionals become more senior, they report reduced support from juniors, increasing individual workload.

**Positive counterpoints cited:**

### 4. Relative Scale Limitations

ELP's headcount (300+ professionals, ~54 partners) is significantly smaller than the Big Six Indian firms. Cyril Amarchand Mangaldas, for example, has 1,200+ lawyers and 220+ partners. This size differential affects ELP's ability to compete for the largest, most complex M&A and capital markets mandates — even when the firm has capable practitioners. Several top Fortune 500 clients that ELP retains for tax and trade work send their larger transactional matters to CAM, AZB, or SAM.
